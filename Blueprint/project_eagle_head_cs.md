# Project Eagle — Head of Customer Success
## Role Proposal & Mandate (Phase-aware)
**Version 1.0 | Confidential**

---

## 1. Context & Strategic Rationale

Project Eagle is an AI-native regulatory reporting factory, purpose-built to serve Alternative Investment Fund Managers (AIFMs) in meeting their obligations under AIFMD Annex IV. The product is sold on trust: clients rely on Project Eagle to ensure their regulatory filings are correct, complete and submitted on time. Every client interaction — from onboarding to renewal — either reinforces or erodes that trust.

The Head of Customer Success is the **guardian of client trust post-contract**. Once a client signs, the CRO's job is done; the Head of Customer Success's job begins. In a subscription business with high CAC and long sales cycles, the economics of retention are not a secondary concern — they are the business model.

The mandate of this role is fourfold:

1. **Onboarding** — get clients live, correctly configured and confident within the shortest viable timeframe
2. **Retention** — ensure clients stay, succeed and renew by monitoring health and resolving issues proactively
3. **Expansion** — identify and enable growth within the existing client base
4. **Voice of the client** — translate client experience into structured input for the product, operations and compliance teams

---

## 2. Strategic Design Choice: Success Before Satisfaction

In regulated markets, customer success means more than satisfaction. An AIFM client who is satisfied but submitting incorrect data is not a success. An AIFM client who is frustrated by onboarding but correctly submitting on time is closer to success than they feel.

> **The measure of customer success at Project Eagle is not how the client feels about the product — it is whether the client is meeting their regulatory obligations correctly and on time, with Eagle.**

This shapes how the role operates. Onboarding is not complete when the client is trained — it is complete when the first submission is correct. Health scoring includes compliance outcomes, not just satisfaction metrics. The Head of Customer Success must develop sufficient regulatory fluency to have credible conversations with AIFM compliance officers.

## 4. Responsibilities (Phase-aware)

### 4.1 Client Onboarding

Onboarding for a regulatory reporting product is not a training exercise — it is a precision integration with real compliance consequences if done incorrectly.

**Phase 1**
- Own the end-to-end onboarding process from contract signature to first successful submission
- Define the onboarding playbook: steps, owners, timelines, acceptance criteria
- Configure each client's profile: AIFM master data, AIF register, NCA registrations, reporting regime flags
- Coordinate client data integration: which ingestion channel(s) the client will use, data format mapping, test submission cycle
- Conduct structured training: data preparer workflows, compliance reviewer workflows, submission approval process
- Define onboarding completion criteria: first submission processed, validated, approved and submitted to NCA without errors

**Phase 2**
- Formal onboarding programme with defined SLA: time from contract to first live submission
- Onboarding health tracking: flag clients at risk of delayed go-live
- Self-service onboarding documentation: client knowledge base, video guides, FAQ
- Onboarding retrospective: structured review of every onboarding to improve the playbook

---

### 4.2 Client Health Monitoring & Retention

**Phase 1**
- Define a client health score incorporating: submission success rate, SLA performance, data quality flags, support ticket frequency, engagement levels, upcoming contract renewal date
- Monitor health score per client; flag deteriorating health to CEO and Head of CS weekly
- Proactive intervention: contact clients showing health warning signs before they raise the issue
- Renewal tracker: maintain visibility of all renewal dates; initiate renewal conversation at least 90 days before expiry

**Phase 2**
- Formal client health dashboard updated after every reporting period
- Segmented health management: standard clients vs. elevated-care clients
- Churn prediction model: identify leading indicators of churn from historical data
- Quarterly business review (QBR) programme for all clients above a defined ARR threshold

---

### 4.3 Expansion Revenue

**Phase 1**
- Identify expansion opportunities within the existing client base: additional AIFs added to scope, additional NCA submissions, new users or access tiers
- Flag expansion opportunities to CRO with supporting context: client has grown their fund count, entered a new NCA jurisdiction, or expressed interest in additional functionality
- Provide commercial support for expansion conversations: usage data, health data, case for value

**Phase 2**
- Formal expansion tracking: per-client expansion opportunity log with ARR estimate and probability
- Expansion motion: Head of CS identifies and develops the relationship; CRO closes the commercial terms
- Expansion ARR reported in sales pipeline alongside new ARR

---

### 4.4 SLA Monitoring — Client Perspective

The COO owns SLA performance from an operational perspective; the Head of Customer Success owns it from the **client perspective**:

| SLA Dimension | Owner |
|---|---|
| Internal SLA performance — pipeline throughput, exception rates, submission success | COO |
| **Client-facing SLA experience — is the client aware of any issues? Are commitments being met?** | **Head of Customer Success** |

**Phase 1**
- Monitor client-facing SLA performance: did each client's submission land on time, without errors, without unplanned intervention required?
- First point of contact for client-reported issues: log, triage and coordinate resolution with COO (operational) or CTO (technical)
- Communicate proactively with clients during any incident affecting their submissions — in coordination with COO

**Phase 2**
- Formal client-facing SLA reporting: monthly service report per client
- Incident communication protocol: standard templates, timing and escalation triggers for client-affecting incidents
- Post-incident client communication: within 5 business days of MAJOR incidents, provide a written summary to affected clients

---

### 4.5 DORA Incident Response — Client Communication

DORA incident management is a shared responsibility. The Head of Customer Success supports the COO in **client communication** during incidents:

| Incident Lane | Owner |
|---|---|
| Technical detection, containment & resolution | CTO |
| Operational impact assessment & SLA management | COO |
| **Client communication during incidents** | **Head of Customer Success (executes, COO-coordinated)** |
| Regulatory notification — DORA Art.18 / NIS2 Art.23 | Head of Compliance & Risk |

**Phase 1**
- Maintain client contact list with primary and secondary contacts for incident communication
- Execute client notifications during MAJOR and SIGNIFICANT incidents as directed by COO
- Track client responses and concerns during incidents; feed back to COO and Head of Compliance & Risk
- Ensure post-incident client follow-up is completed within the defined timeframe

**Phase 2**
- Formal client communication runbook for each incident severity level
- Client notification templates: pre-approved, accurate, compliant with DORA client notification obligations
- Post-incident client satisfaction check: structured follow-up after MAJOR incidents

---

### 4.6 Client-Level Risk — Relationship & Satisfaction Dimension

Each client represents a distinct risk profile across multiple dimensions. The Head of Customer Success owns the **relationship and satisfaction risk**:

| Client Risk Type | Owner |
|---|---|
| Technical risk — data model mismatch, integration instability | CTO |
| Operational risk — SLA breach, pipeline failure | COO |
| Regulatory risk — incorrect submission, NCA error | Head of Compliance & Risk |
| Financial risk — revenue concentration, payment default | CFO |
| Commercial risk — contract misalignment, scope dispute | CRO |
| **Relationship risk — dissatisfaction, low engagement, churn signal** | **Head of Customer Success** |

**Phase 1**
- Maintain a per-client relationship health assessment: engagement level, satisfaction signals, key stakeholder relationships
- Flag clients where relationship health is deteriorating — even if operational metrics are green
- Identify dependency risk: clients over-reliant on a single internal contact who may leave

**Phase 2**
- Multi-stakeholder relationship mapping per client: ensure Eagle has relationships at compliance, operations and management level — not just one person
- Formal churn risk register updated monthly

---

### 4.7 Voice of the Client — Client Experience Constraints

The CTO/CPO owns the product roadmap. The Head of Customer Success's role is to define **client experience constraints** — the friction points, workflow failures and retention risks that the product must address to keep clients succeeding. These are constraints presented with evidence; the CTO/CPO decides whether and when to act on them.

**Phase 1**
- Maintain a structured client constraint log: workflow friction, onboarding obstacles, recurring support issues — each tagged with frequency, client count affected, and estimated retention impact
- Monthly session with CTO/CPO: present the client evidence — this issue affects X clients, generates Y support tickets per period, and has been cited in Z health score deteriorations — not a feature wishlist
- Distinguish between: (a) genuine product constraints affecting multiple clients, (b) configuration needs already addressable, and (c) client-specific issues that do not indicate a product problem
- Feed compliance questions and regulatory interpretation queries to Head of Compliance & Risk — do not self-adjudicate

**Phase 2**
- Formal NPS / CSAT programme: quarterly measurement, trend tracking, segmented by client size and tenure
- Annual client advisory input: structured engagement with 3–5 clients — findings presented as constraints to CTO/CPO, not as a client-driven roadmap
- Churn analysis: what product or experience factors contributed to churn? Structured evidence shared with CRO and CTO/CPO as commercial and client constraints respectively

---

### 4.8 Training, Documentation & Self-Service

**Phase 1**
- Produce client-facing documentation: user guides, onboarding checklists, FAQ, data format specifications
- Conduct training sessions for each client at onboarding; define refresher triggers (new users, new AIFs, new NCAs)
- Ensure documentation is accurate, versioned and accessible — tied to product releases

**Phase 2**
- Client knowledge base: searchable, self-service documentation covering all standard workflows
- Video training library: key workflows recorded and accessible in the client portal
- Reduce dependency on Head of CS for routine questions — self-service is a scalability mechanism

## 5. Structural Position

```
CEO
 ├── CRO  →  acquisition + commercial
 │    ↔ (renewal coordination)
 └── Head of Customer Success  →  onboarding + retention + expansion
      ├── coordinates with: COO (operational SLA), CTO (technical issues)
      ├── coordinates with: Head of Compliance & Risk (regulatory queries)
      └── feeds back to: CTO/CPO (product), COO (process), CFO (health / churn risk)
```

**Phase 1 combination:** CRO or COO covers interim. The Head of CS hire should happen **at first client go-live** — not when the CS backlog becomes unmanageable.

**Hire rationale:** In RegTech, the first client onboarding is the most important product moment. A poor first onboarding experience — delayed go-live, incorrect configuration, failed first submission — creates a reference account problem and a churn risk simultaneously.

**Three Lines of Defence:** The Head of Customer Success is not a governance role. However, they are the team's most direct window into whether the product is performing as promised in a real client context. Observations from client interactions that suggest compliance, data quality or product integrity issues must be escalated promptly to Head of Compliance & Risk.

## 7. Guiding Principles

> **Principle 1 — Customer experience first:** This is the Head of Customer Success's primary mandate — not as a slogan, but as a measurable standard. Correct, on-time submissions every period, with minimal friction, is the bar.

> **Principle 3 — Standardised product with predictable output:** The Head of CS reinforces this with clients. Client requests for bespoke features are logged and fed to the product team — not committed to.

> **Principle 7 — Resilience:** Every client-facing process — onboarding, health monitoring, incident communication and renewal management — has a documented fallback owner and a backup procedure. The DORA client communication lane must be staffed and executable regardless of personnel availability; a single point of failure in this lane creates a compliance risk for the AIFM client. Response runbooks, contact registers and communication templates are maintained and tested so that the lane operates independently of any one person.

> **Principle 14 — Database-first:** Client health scores, renewal dates, expansion pipeline, onboarding progress, NPS/CSAT measurements and support ticket history are stored in the database as first-class records. Dashboards and client reports are generated views of database data, never standalone spreadsheets or file extracts. No health score or renewal risk assessment is authoritative unless it derives from the canonical database record.

> **Principle 10 — Founder-independent by design:** Onboarding processes, training materials and client documentation must be fully systematised. Client success cannot depend on institutional memory held by one person.

> **Principle 11 — Continuous learning system:** Every client interaction — every support ticket, every feedback comment, every churn event — is data. The Head of CS ensures this data reaches the teams who can act on it.

> **Principle 12 — Ambitious growth and profitability:** Retention is the highest-return activity in a subscription business. Improving gross retention by 10 percentage points compounds faster than the same improvement in new ARR.

> **Principle 13 — Internationally oriented:** The Head of Customer Success serves a pan-European AIFM client base operating across multiple NCA jurisdictions. All client documentation, onboarding materials, training content and incident communications are in English. The CS function is built for multi-jurisdiction operation from day one — no assumptions of a single-NCA or single-language client base. Onboarding playbooks and communication runbooks accommodate jurisdictional variation in reporting calendars and NCA requirements.

> **Principle 16 — Location-independent by design:** Client onboarding, training, health monitoring and incident communication all operate remotely by default. Client documentation, onboarding checklists and communication runbooks must be fully self-contained — any team member must be able to manage a client relationship from any location without dependency on shared physical context.

## 8. Key Customer Success Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) | % of ARR retained at renewal excluding expansion | Head of CS |
| Net Revenue Retention (NRR) | GRR + expansion ARR / prior period ARR | Head of CS + CRO |
| Time to first live submission | Days from contract signature to first successful NCA submission | Head of CS |
| Client health score (average) | Average health score across all active clients | Head of CS |
| Clients at risk (count / ARR) | Clients with red health score | Head of CS (reports to CEO) |
| NPS / CSAT | Net promoter score; customer satisfaction score | Head of CS |
| Expansion ARR identified | Pipeline of expansion opportunities identified by CS | Head of CS (reports to CRO) |
| Support ticket resolution time | Average time to resolve client-reported issues | Head of CS (with COO / CTO) |

## 9. Key Insight

> In a regulated market, client success is not measured by satisfaction surveys — it is measured by correct submissions, on-time, every period. Everything else is a proxy.
