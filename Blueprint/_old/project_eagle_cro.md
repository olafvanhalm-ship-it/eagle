# Project Eagle — Chief Revenue Officer (CRO)
## Role Proposal & Mandate (Phase-aware)
**Version 1.0 | Confidential**

---

## 1. Context & Strategic Rationale

Project Eagle is an AI-native regulatory reporting factory, purpose-built to serve Alternative Investment Fund Managers (AIFMs) in meeting their obligations under AIFMD Annex IV. The commercial model is B2B subscription: the CRO's job is to acquire clients who will stay, expand and refer — not to close deals that create delivery problems or bespoke obligations.

The CRO is the **revenue engine** of Project Eagle — but in a regulated, trust-intensive market, how revenue is generated matters as much as how much. A single poorly-scoped deal that forces bespoke development, compromises the standardised product model, or attracts a client whose needs exceed what the product can deliver is more damaging than a missed quarter.

The mandate of this role is fourfold:

1. **Pipeline ownership** — build and manage the commercial pipeline from first contact to signed contract
2. **Scope discipline** — protect the standardised product model from commercial pressure to customise
3. **Revenue quality** — acquire clients with the right profile: regulatory fit, operational readiness, and expansion potential
4. **Commercial intelligence** — translate market signals into structured product input for CTO/CPO, without bespoke commitments

---

## 2. Strategic Design Choice: Sell the Standard, Not the Exception

Project Eagle's commercial model is built on a standardised product. This is its margin engine, its scalability mechanism, and its competitive differentiator — clients get a proven, auditable system, not a bespoke build. The CRO must internalise and enforce this principle in every commercial conversation:

> **The CRO's job is to find clients who fit the product — not to reshape the product to fit the client.**

This requires a different commercial mindset from traditional enterprise sales. Discovery is as much about disqualification as qualification. Scope discussions happen early, not late. The CRO is the first line of protection against scope creep — not the person who introduces it.

## 4. Responsibilities (Phase-aware)

### 4.1 Sales Strategy & Pipeline Management

**Phase 1** *(post-hire — CEO covers prior)*
- Define the Ideal Client Profile (ICP) in detail: AIFM size, AIF count, NCA footprint, reporting complexity
- Build and maintain a pipeline management system: stages, probabilities, expected close dates
- Own outreach, qualification, demos, negotiation and closing for all new business
- Report pipeline status to CEO weekly: opportunities by stage, expected ARR, blockers

**Phase 2**
- Formal sales process documentation: playbook, scripts, objection handling, competitive positioning
- Pipeline review cadence: weekly team review, monthly CEO review
- Hire and manage Sales Development Representatives (SDRs) when pipeline volume demands it
- Sales forecasting: quarterly and annual ARR targets with confidence-weighted pipeline

---

### 4.2 Client Acquisition: From Prospect to Signed Contract

**Phase 1**
- Manage the full acquisition cycle: prospecting, outreach, discovery, demo, proposal, negotiation, close
- Discovery protocol: understand the prospect's current reporting process, pain points, NCA footprint, team structure, compliance maturity
- Disqualification discipline: identify early whether a prospect requires bespoke functionality that the product does not support — exit gracefully rather than over-promise
- Commercial contract structuring in coordination with external legal counsel; ensure contracts include DORA Art.30 required provisions (coordinated with Head of Compliance & Risk)

**Phase 2**
- Enterprise sales motion for larger AIFMs: multi-stakeholder engagement (compliance, IT, operations, legal)
- RFP/RFI response capability for institutional procurement processes
- Reference client programme: structured use of early clients to accelerate later cycles

---

### 4.3 Pricing Strategy & Financial Guardrails

Pricing strategy is owned jointly by the CRO (market-facing) and the CFO (financial floor). The CRO operates within the financial guardrails set by the CFO:

| Pricing Dimension | Owner |
|---|---|
| Market pricing strategy — positioning, tiering, competitive response | CRO |
| Pricing floor — minimum price at which a deal is financially viable | CFO |
| Volume discount thresholds — maximum discount by deal size | CFO (sets threshold) / CRO (applies within limit) |
| Pricing exceptions — deals below floor | CEO approval required |

**Phase 1**
- Define the pricing model in coordination with CEO and CFO: per-AIF, per-NCA, volume tiers, or hybrid
- Test pricing with early prospects; iterate based on market response
- Never commit to pricing below the CFO-defined floor without explicit CEO approval

**Phase 2**
- Formal pricing governance: quarterly pricing review with CEO, CFO and CTO
- Competitive pricing intelligence: track market positioning relative to alternatives
- Multi-year contract structures with defined escalation clauses

---

### 4.4 Scope Discipline — Protecting the Standard Product

The CRO is the **first line of scope protection**. Commercial pressure to customise must be identified and managed in the sales process — not discovered post-contract by the CTO or COO.

**Phase 1**
- Maintain a clear written description of what the standard product covers and what it does not
- Surface all client requirements during discovery; flag any requirement outside standard scope to CEO and CTO before proposal stage
- Never commit to bespoke development, custom integrations or non-standard workflows in a commercial proposal without explicit CTO and CEO sign-off
- If a prospect's requirements cannot be met by the standard product, escalate to CEO — do not close a deal that requires capability the product does not have

**Phase 2**
- Formal scope qualification checklist: every deal signed off against standard product boundary before contract
- Configuration vs. customisation escalation protocol with CTO
- Regular feedback to CTO/CPO on recurring prospect requirements that indicate genuine product gaps vs. one-off bespoke demands

---

### 4.5 Commercial Constraints for the Product Roadmap

The CTO/CPO owns the product roadmap. The CRO's role is to define the **commercial constraints** within which that roadmap must operate — not to direct it. The distinction matters: the CRO articulates what the market requires and what the commercial cost of inaction is; the CTO/CPO decides what to build and when.

**Phase 1**
- Maintain a commercial constraint log: every repeated capability gap, qualifying objection and competitive loss attributed to product, tagged by frequency and ICP fit
- Monthly structured constraint session with CTO/CPO: present the commercial evidence — these gaps are affecting X deals per quarter with Y ARR at stake — and let the CTO/CPO prioritise
- Distinguish clearly between: (a) genuine product constraints affecting multiple ICP-fit prospects, (b) configuration needs already addressable, and (c) one-off bespoke requests that do not represent market demand
- Never commit to a product capability in a commercial conversation without explicit CTO/CPO confirmation that it is on the roadmap and when

**Phase 2**
- Formal commercial constraint register: maintained by CRO, reviewed quarterly with CTO/CPO
- Win/loss analysis: structured post-mortem on every lost deal — product constraint, pricing, timing or wrong ICP — fed into the constraint register, not the roadmap directly

---

### 4.6 Renewal Coordination with Head of Customer Success

New ARR and retained ARR are both commercial outcomes. The CRO owns acquisition; the Head of Customer Success owns retention and expansion. Renewal is the handoff:

| Revenue Type | Owner |
|---|---|
| New ARR | CRO |
| Expansion ARR (additional AIFs, NCAs, users) | Head of CS (identifies) + CRO (contracts) |
| Renewal | Head of CS (relationship) + CRO (commercial terms) |
| Churn prevention | Head of CS (primary) — CRO engaged if churn risk is commercial in nature |

**Phase 1**
- Maintain visibility of all contract renewal dates; flag renewals at risk to Head of CS and CEO
- Provide commercial support for expansion conversations identified by Head of CS

**Phase 2**
- Formal renewal and expansion process: CS identifies opportunity, CRO closes
- Renewal forecast included in sales reporting
- NPS / satisfaction data from Head of CS used as input to renewal risk assessment

---

### 4.7 Client-Level Commercial Risk

Each client represents a distinct commercial risk profile. The CRO owns **commercial risk per client**:

| Client Risk Type | Owner |
|---|---|
| Operational risk — SLA breach, pipeline failure | COO |
| Technical risk — data model mismatch, integration instability | CTO |
| Regulatory risk — incorrect submission, NCA error | Head of Compliance & Risk |
| Financial risk — revenue concentration, payment default | CFO |
| **Commercial risk — contract misalignment, scope dispute, renewal risk** | **CRO** |

**Phase 1**
- Flag any client where the contracted scope is approaching a boundary that the product cannot support — before it becomes a delivery problem
- Identify clients with low usage, low engagement or low satisfaction as renewal risk — escalate to Head of CS and CEO
- Ensure all contracts contain clear scope definitions that protect Eagle from obligation creep post-signature

**Phase 2**
- Formal commercial risk assessment for each client at renewal
- Contract audit: ensure all active contracts are consistent with current product capabilities and pricing model

---

### 4.8 Contract Compliance with DORA Art.30

As an ICT supplier to regulated AIFM clients, Eagle's client contracts must comply with DORA Article 30 minimum provisions. The CRO is responsible for ensuring every contract meets this standard:

**Phase 1**
- Maintain the DORA Art.30 contractual information pack (produced by Head of Compliance & Risk; CRO ensures it is included in every client contract)
- Never sign a client contract that omits required DORA provisions — flag to Head of Compliance & Risk if a client's legal team requests removal or amendment
- Ensure service level, incident notification and audit right provisions are consistent with Eagle's DORA obligations

**Phase 2**
- Annual contract review against DORA requirements (coordinated with Head of Compliance & Risk)
- Standard contract template maintained as a versioned document, updated with each regulatory change

## 5. Structural Position

```
CEO
 ├── CRO  →  pipeline, acquisition, contracts, pricing  [Phase 1: CEO covers]
 │    └── SDR / Sales team  →  Phase 2 hires as pipeline scales
 ├── CTO / CPO
 ├── COO
 ├── CFO
 └── Head of Compliance & Risk
```

**Phase 1 combination:** CEO carries CRO responsibilities. Split trigger: ~€500K ARR or when sales cycle complexity requires full-time focus.

**Key structural relationships:**
- **CRO → CFO:** pricing floor, financial guardrails, revenue reporting
- **CRO → CTO/CPO:** product feedback, scope qualification, feature pipeline
- **CRO → Head of CS:** renewal coordination, expansion ARR, client health signals
- **CRO → Head of Compliance & Risk:** DORA Art.30 contract compliance, regulatory constraints in commercial terms
- **CRO → CEO:** pipeline reporting, pricing exceptions, strategic account escalation

The CRO does not have a governance / Three Lines of Defence role. Commercial integrity is maintained through structural separation: the CRO cannot commit to technical, operational or compliance capabilities that are not verified by CTO, COO or Head of Compliance & Risk respectively.

## 7. Guiding Principles

> **Principle 2 — Code-first, human-by-exception:** All processes run through a central orchestration layer; humans intervene only for exceptions, judgement and improvement. The CRO sells this principle to every prospect: Eagle is an automated reporting factory, not a managed service with manual steps. Prospects who expect human-operated workflows as part of the standard product are ICP mismatches and must be disqualified early in discovery — not managed around with post-contract workarounds.

> **Principle 1 — Customer experience first:** The CRO sets client expectations accurately in the sales process. Overpromising to close a deal is a customer experience failure — it just manifests post-contract.

> **Principle 3 — Standardised product with predictable output:** The CRO is the commercial guardian of this principle. Every deal should fit the standard product; every exception requires CEO and CTO sign-off.

> **Principle 12 — Ambitious growth and profitability:** >€1M ARR is the Phase 2 target. The CRO's pipeline and close rates are the primary lever. But growth that destroys margin or compromises product integrity is not ambitious — it is reckless.

> **Principle 13 — Internationally oriented:** The AIFM market is pan-European and global. The CRO builds a pipeline that reflects Eagle's international ambitions from the outset — not a pipeline limited to one NCA jurisdiction.

> **Principle 16 — Location-independent by design:** Sales processes, client demos, contract negotiations and pipeline management all operate remotely by default. The CRO builds a commercial motion that works across time zones and jurisdictions — a virtual office is a natural fit for a pan-European AIFM client base.
