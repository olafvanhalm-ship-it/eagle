# Project Eagle — Chief Executive Officer (CEO)
## Role Proposal & Mandate (Phase-aware)
**Version 1.0 | Confidential**

---

## 1. Context & Strategic Rationale

Project Eagle is an AI-native regulatory reporting factory, purpose-built to serve Alternative Investment Fund Managers (AIFMs) in meeting their obligations under AIFMD Annex IV. The company operates in a trust-intensive market: clients are regulated financial institutions whose own compliance officers will scrutinise the product before adoption, and whose continued use depends on reliability and regulatory integrity.

The CEO is the **architect of the whole** — the role that sets strategic direction, allocates capital, owns the most consequential external relationships, and ensures that the commercial, technology, operations and compliance functions operate in coherent alignment rather than productive tension.

The mandate of this role is fourfold:

1. **Strategy and capital** — set direction, allocate resources, manage investors and the board
2. **Key relationships** — own the relationships that cannot be delegated: top-tier clients, strategic partners, regulators
3. **Organisational alignment** — ensure the leadership team is structured, incentivised and operating as a coherent whole
4. **Ultimate accountability** — hold final accountability for governance, ethics and the company's obligations to its clients

---

## 2. Strategic Design Choice: Founder Sells First

In B2B markets serving regulated institutions, the sales cycle is long (typically 6–18 months), trust-dependent, and disproportionately influenced by who is in the room. In Phase 1, the CEO must personally lead commercial relationships:

> **The founder is the product in early sales. Credibility, regulatory fluency and commitment are evaluated before the product is.**

This is not a compromise — it is the optimal go-to-market strategy for Phase 1. Delegating early sales to a non-founder typically extends cycles and reduces close rates in institutional markets. The CRO hire is a Phase 1.5 decision, triggered by pipeline volume — not by the CEO's preference to step back.

## 4. Responsibilities (Phase-aware)

### 4.1 Company Strategy & Market Positioning

**Phase 1**
- Define and articulate the company strategy: target market, product positioning, competitive moat
- Set the three-year financial ambition and the milestones that anchor it (cash flow positive within 2 years, >€1M ARR, >50% EBITDA margin)
- Make the key early product scope decisions: what is in the standardised product, what is not
- Ensure the company is oriented towards the AIFM segment and not distracted by adjacent opportunities

**Phase 2**
- Annual strategy review: market position, competitive dynamics, expansion options (new segments, new regulatory frameworks, new geographies)
- Define the criteria for adjacent product or market moves — and the criteria for staying focused
- Horizon scanning: AIFMD 2.0, ESG reporting mandates, potential new regulatory frameworks

---

### 4.2 Fundraising, Capital Allocation & Board Management

**Phase 1**
- Manage runway: in coordination with CFO, ensure the company never faces a funding crisis without at least 6 months' visibility
- Maintain investor-readiness at all times — financial records, pitch materials, cap table (coordinated with CFO)
- Make capital allocation decisions: where to invest (engineering vs. sales vs. operations) given the current phase

**Phase 2**
- Lead Series A fundraising process (with CFO managing financial due diligence)
- Establish formal board: composition, cadence, governance
- Investor reporting: quarterly board pack, transparent communication of risks and opportunities

**Advisory structure**
From Phase 1, establish an informal advisory board with expertise in: AIFMD regulatory practice, institutional sales into asset management, and enterprise SaaS operations. This is market-standard for RegTech companies and accelerates credibility with AIFM compliance buyers.

---

### 4.3 Commercial Leadership — Phase 1 CRO Responsibilities

Until the CRO hire, the CEO owns the full commercial function:

**Phase 1**
- Own the sales pipeline: prospecting, qualification, demos, negotiation and closing
- Manage pricing decisions within the financial guardrails set by the CFO (pricing floor, margin minimum)
- Structure commercial contracts in coordination with external legal counsel
- Establish the commercial playbook: ICP definition, outreach cadence, demo script, objection handling
- Feed structured product feedback to CTO/CPO — without committing to bespoke development

**Transition to CRO**
- Hire CRO at ~€500K ARR or when pipeline volume exceeds single-person management capacity
- Transfer pipeline, playbook, client relationships and commercial process to CRO with a defined handover period
- CEO retains relationship ownership for strategic accounts post-hire

---

### 4.4 Key Relationships & External Representation

**Phase 1**
- Relationship owner for all early AIFM clients — transition to Head of Customer Success at go-live, but CEO retains strategic oversight
- Primary contact for strategic data partners and integration providers
- External representation: industry bodies (EFAMA, ALFI, etc.), conferences, press

**Phase 2**
- Relationship owner for top 5 clients by ARR — these accounts require CEO-level engagement
- Regulatory relationship management: proactive contact with relevant NCAs (AFM, CSSF, etc.) as Eagle establishes market presence
- Partnership development: strategic integrations (fund administrators, depositary banks, data providers)

---

### 4.5 Organisational Alignment & Leadership Team

**Phase 1**
- Define the hiring sequence: which roles to fill in which order, and why
- Ensure no role combination persists beyond its appropriate trigger — combinations are time-limited workarounds, not permanent structures
- Facilitate cross-role alignment: where ownership overlaps (DORA, data, AI), the CEO is the escalation point for unresolved conflicts

**Phase 2**
- Formal leadership team cadence: weekly operational review, monthly financial review, quarterly strategy review
- Performance framework: OKRs or equivalent — tied to the company's phase targets, not vanity metrics
- Culture and values: define what Project Eagle stands for beyond the product

---

### 4.6 Ultimate Governance Accountability

The CEO holds final accountability for the governance model — including the Three Lines of Defence structure, the AI ethics boundary, and the company's obligations as an ICT supplier under DORA.

| Governance Domain | Day-to-day owner | CEO's role |
|---|---|---|
| Compliance framework | Head of Compliance & Risk | Approve risk appetite; escalation point for unresolved regulatory decisions |
| Financial controls | CFO | Approve financial thresholds; sign off on major investments |
| Technical integrity | CTO | Approve architecture decisions with material business implications |
| Operational performance | COO | Receive operational reporting; intervene on systemic SLA failures |
| AI ethics | Head of Compliance & Risk (oversight) + CTO (design) | Hold final accountability; cannot delegate ethical responsibility |

**Phase 1**
- Ensure the Three Lines of Defence model is operational from launch — not retrofitted
- Approve the risk register and escalation thresholds maintained by Head of Compliance & Risk
- Sign off on any decision to deploy AI in a new use case within the pipeline

**Phase 2**
- Board-level governance: present compliance, risk and AI governance posture to board quarterly
- External accountability: as Eagle grows, the CEO may be called upon to represent the company's governance model to NCA clients or regulators directly

## 5. Structural Position

The CEO sits at the apex of the structure, with direct reports covering all five functional domains:

```
CEO
 ├── CTO / CPO  →  system + product          [1st line: technical controls]
 ├── COO        →  factory operations         [1st line: operational controls]
 ├── CFO        →  financial control          [financial thresholds + 3rd line coordination]
 ├── CRO        →  revenue + commercial       [Phase 1: CEO covers]
 ├── Head of CS →  client success             [Phase 1: CRO or COO covers]
 └── Head of Compliance & Risk               [2nd line: independent oversight — standalone]
```

**Structural principles:**
- Commercial pressure (CEO/CRO) is structurally separated from product integrity (CTO) and compliance (Head of Compliance & Risk)
- The Head of Compliance & Risk has a direct line to the CEO — not mediated through COO or CTO
- No role is permanently combined; all Phase 1 combinations have explicit split triggers

## 7. Guiding Principles

> **Principle 1 — Customer experience first:** Simplicity, reliability and predictability over customisation. The CEO sets the tone: the product is what it is, and what it is must be excellent.

> **Principle 2 — Code-first, human-by-exception:** All processes run through a central orchestration layer; humans intervene only for exceptions, judgement and improvement. The CEO is ultimately accountable for building an organisation that runs on codified processes, not on heroic individual effort. This principle is the operational expression of Principle 10 — a founder-independent company is one where the system does the work, not the people.

> **Principle 3 — Standardised product with predictable output:** The CEO enforces scope discipline at the strategic level — no bespoke commitments made in sales that the product cannot deliver.

> **Principle 10 — Founder-independent by design:** All knowledge, rules and processes are fully codified in systems. The CEO is the primary owner of this principle: if the company depends on any individual — including the CEO — something has been built wrong.

> **Principle 12 — Ambitious growth and profitability:** Cash flow positive within two years, >€1M ARR and >50% EBITDA margin. The CEO is ultimately accountable for achieving this — not just for setting it as a target.

> **Principle 13 — Internationally oriented:** Products offered worldwide; all communication in English. The CEO sets the international ambition and ensures the product, team and commercial model are built for it from the start.

> **Principle 16 — Location-independent by design:** Eagle operates as a virtual office — no physical headquarters required. The CEO makes this a structural commitment, not a working preference: hiring decisions, team cadence, communication norms and governance processes are all designed to function from any location. Presence is never a proxy for performance or trust.

## 8. Key Decisions the CEO Must Make (and Cannot Delegate)

| Decision | Why it cannot be delegated |
|---|---|
| Which clients to say no to | Scope discipline must come from the top |
| When to raise external funding and on what terms | Determines the company's trajectory and founder dilution |
| When to separate combined roles | Phase 1 combinations persist too long without CEO-led forcing functions |
| Risk appetite — what regulatory exposure is acceptable | Ultimate accountability is non-delegable |
| When to enter new markets or regulatory frameworks | Strategic direction is the CEO's core function |
| Who to hire into the leadership team | Culture and capability are set by the first 5–8 hires |

---

## 9. Key Insight

> The CEO's most important product is the organisation itself. Every hiring decision, every governance choice, every commercial commitment is a design decision about what Project Eagle will be capable of at scale.
